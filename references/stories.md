# Stories guide — YB Vet

## Why stories work

Short, **composite** moments help owners see themselves in the writing — without exposing any real client.

**Style benchmark (internal only):** The parent group’s **other** public blogs (used only as a scraped pattern reference) favour the same **story grammar**: relatable trigger → tension → resolution → clear takeaway. Match that **shape and warmth** on YB Vet with **fresh wording** every time. **Never** name or link the sister business in published YB Vet posts.

## Privacy rules (non-negotiable)

- **Composite only:** “A dog we saw recently…” / “Owners often tell us…”
- **No** real names, streets, workplaces, or unusual identifying details.
- **Never** imply a story is about a **current** identifiable patient.
- When in doubt, **broaden** the anecdote: “This is something we see a lot in small-breed dogs.”

## Story template

1. **Trigger** — what brought them in (often something small or delayed).  
2. **Assumption** — what the owner feared or believed.  
3. **What happened** — exam, plan, outcome (can be “reassuringly normal”).  
4. **Turn outward** — what the reader might do (book, monitor, bring records); for true emergencies, point to **“your nearest 24-hour emergency veterinary hospital”** (generic) unless leadership has approved specific partner wording on this domain.

**Length:** about **80–120 words**. **Maximum one story** per blog post. **Rewrite in fresh words** each time — do not recycle the same paragraph across posts.

## Story themes (aligned with benchmark pacing, YB Vet content)

1. **The “fine until they weren’t” visit** — older pet, subtle weight loss, owner surprised by what the exam or bloods showed.  
2. **The nervous first visit** — puppy or rescue; floor-level exam, owner relieved it wasn’t a battle.  
3. **The cat who “hates the vet”** — carrier training + quiet handling; owner says the cat was calmer than expected.  
4. **The Saturday morning slot** — working owner grateful for a half-day window.  
5. **The “is this an emergency?” moment** — owner unsure; calm triage-style explanation of what **this** clinic can do today vs what needs **emergency hospital** care (**no** naming of sister sites unless approved).  
6. **Seasonal / household moment** — heat, holidays, toxins on the counter — **scene-first** like the benchmark, then **YB Vet–accurate** advice.

## Placement

Best as **second or third paragraph**, after the direct answer to the reader’s question.
